# Cover Letters

The applicant's letters have a recognizable architecture. Use it. The cover letter is the *opposite* game from the resume: where the resume mirrors JD language for ATS, the cover letter **never** mirrors it — paraphrase and add a personal claim.

## The four components

1. **Philosophical hook intro.** Open with an idea or reframe specific to the company's problem space — not "I am writing to apply for…". (e.g., for a surgery-robotics role: "Surgery is the highest-stakes user interface there is.") Don't bury the lead.
2. **"Some Risks to Address."** Name the role-specific risks *directly* rather than hiding them — leading with vulnerability to control the narrative (per Adam Grant's research). Name the gap, then reframe or close it with evidence.
3. **"How My Background Maps to the Role" (evidence body).** Bullet headlines lead with the **most-credentialing element first** for the relevant domain (e.g., software-design → a shipped product or enterprise service-design project; physical-design → the hardware hero project or the MID; PM → large-org business scale or 0→1 venture), followed by a compressed headline + STAR body.
4. **"Why [Company], Why Now" closer.** Must add a **personal claim** — never echo the JD back.

**Ordering note:** Recent letters often place "Some Risks to Address" *after* the fit/evidence section rather than first — both orderings appear. Fixed requirements: the risks section always exists, and the philosophy is always lead-with-vulnerability. Pick the order that flows for the specific letter.

## Standard risk reframes (reuse)

- **Founder = flight risk?** → "I've spent enough time on solo islands to know I do my best work on a team with a mission bigger than me. I'm not looking for a stepping stone; I'm looking for a place to apply what I've learned at the scale where it actually matters."
- **Title gap (PM / non-design titles)** → "'Product Manager' isn't on my title history — but the work is."
- **Pivoting into a new specialty** → willing to step back to an IC foundation and earn the senior role rather than borrow it from another discipline.
- **Location** → single sentence: in [the applicant's location], open to relocation if applicable, prepared for office schedule and travel.
- **Graduate-school risk is omitted by default** unless the role specifically frames against it.

## Domain tailoring of the hook + evidence

- **PM/Business:** hook on the product/business tension the company faces; evidence leads with business judgment at scale + 0→1 ownership.
- **Software Design:** hook on the experience/interface problem; evidence leads with shipped digital products + design-systems craft.
- **Physical Design:** hook on the physical/human-factors problem; evidence leads with the hardware hero project + quantified ergonomics.

## Tone & language rules

- Short, confident prose. No explanatory padding. No consultative or marketing-adjacent phrasing.
- **Never mirror JD language.** Quoting the posting back reads as defensive and pretentious. Paraphrase and add a personal claim. (Opposite of the resume rule.)
- Direct framing of risk over spin.

## Formatting / output

- **One page**, always.
- Typeface **Source Sans 3** (body and headings), matching the resume system; **Helvetica Neue** as the zero-install fallback. Section headers in **petrol-teal #005C6E**, title case; evergreen body near-black.
- Final letter lives in the cover-letter template `.docx` (Source Sans 3, petrol-teal title-case headers, bracketed placeholders in italic teal-gray to replace per role); export a **tagged PDF with embedded fonts**. Claude drafts the prose; the applicant drops it into the template. Don't produce Word/PDF from scratch unless asked.
- Sign-off: "Warm regards, [Your Name] / [Credentials] / [yourwebsite.com] | [linkedin]".

## Anti-pattern to avoid

- ❌ "I am excited to apply for the Senior Product Designer role at [Company]."
- ✅ A one-line reframe of the company's core problem that earns attention before any credential appears.
