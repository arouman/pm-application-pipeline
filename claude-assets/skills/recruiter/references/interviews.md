# Interview Preparation

Two layers: **behavioral** (shared across all roles) and **domain-specific** (one of three loops). At intake, confirm the company, role, domain, and which round this is, then prep the matching layer. Always ground answers in real dossier stories — never invent a STAR.

## Behavioral (all domains)

The bar rose in 2026: a two-minute STAR answer is now the *opening*, followed by 3–5 probing follow-ups ("What was the exact metric? How did you know you were right? What would you do differently? Who disagreed and why?"). Prep for the drill-down, not just the headline.

**Method:** STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result), outcome-weighted. Spend the most time on Action + Result, quantify the Result, and have the *next layer* of detail ready for each story.

**Core story bank (pull from `projects.md` — map each to common prompts):**
- **Lead through ambiguity / 0→1:** the 0→1 venture entry — led a small team, raised capital, navigated a pivot to positive revenue. Or any of the shipped AI products built from a blank page.
- **Influence without authority / cross-functional at scale:** the large-org enterprise project — multi-stakeholder rollout across shared service centers at scale.
- **Data-driven decision / measuring impact:** the large quantitative study entry (n≈10,000 feeding multi-year roadmaps); or the ergonomic study (anthropometrics → quantified population impact).
- **Conflict / disagreement:** pick a real instance from a stakeholder-heavy project or a venture; name the tension, the tradeoff, the resolution, the outcome.
- **Failure / what you'd do differently:** a real pivot or a prototype that failed in testing (a project with many iterations is honest material) — show the learning loop.
- **Mentorship / leadership:** recruited and mentored a team member; TA'd a course and built curriculum.
- **Speed / bias to action:** shipped AI-native products in weeks via Claude Code; "I move at the speed of the week, not the quarter."

**Why-this-company / why-now:** a genuine personal claim, never recycled JD language (same rule as the cover letter). Tie to the company's actual problem and the applicant's trajectory.

**Smart questions to ask back (2026):** how roadmaps actually get built; what excites the team about its direction; how the company stays current on AI. Specific, team-real questions beat generic culture questions.

**Guardrail:** answers must trace to the dossier. Don't let interview pressure manufacture a metric. If a likely question has no real story behind it, build the prep around the closest *true* story and flag the gap to the applicant.

## Domain 1 — Product Management loop

2026 PM loops at top companies run a recruiter screen → PM/HM phone screen → 4–6 onsite rounds, separating these signals:

1. **Product sense / design.** "Design a product for X" / "Improve [product]." Structure: clarify the goal → segment users → surface pain points → prioritize → propose solutions → define success metrics. **2026 shift:** think in workflows and AI-native features, not just screens. Frameworks (JTBD, etc.) held loosely — interviewers want adaptability, not recitation.
2. **Execution / analytical (metrics).** North-star + leading/lagging metrics, funnel breakdowns, hypothesis generation, A/B logic. **2026 shift:** the **conflicting-metric tradeoff** ("two metrics pull opposite ways — what do you do?") has largely replaced the classic "diagnose the 20% drop" funnel question. Expect hard pushback ("No — go deeper"); show systematic root-cause coverage (bug, UI change, rollout, behavior shift, seasonality, external) before narrowing.
3. **AI product sense (its own pillar now).** A normal product prompt, then **vibe-code/prototype a solution with AI tools live**, while the interviewer grills on token usage, latency, retrieval, and evals — *even in non-AI roles*. **This is the applicant's edge if they have shipped AI products.** Their shipped AI products, orchestration experience, and provider/usage fluency are directly on-point. Prep them to narrate guiding/critiquing AI output, not writing code line-by-line.
4. **Leadership & drive (behavioral).** STAR + deep follow-ups (above), outcome- and growth-focused.
5. **Technical depth (for PM-T or AI roles).** Drill-downs on tokens, retrieval, evals, system architecture at the *fluency* level — never claiming they wrote the backend.

**Estimation and in-person whiteboarding have mostly faded** at the top firms — don't over-index on Fermi estimation unless the company specifically uses it.

**Positioning:** lean on the title-gap reframe early; convert "no PM title" into "I've shipped, prioritized, and owned outcomes — most recently AI-native, weekly." Map every answer to discovery/delivery/leadership balance.

## Domain 2 — Software / Digital Product Design loop

Standard top-company loop: recruiter screen → portfolio preview → onsite of 4–5 rounds. The portfolio is the spine (90% of hiring managers weight it heavily); many top teams have **de-emphasized live whiteboard tests** in favor of deep portfolio + past-work interviews (Faire's Craft / Execution / Product Sense / Design Citizenship rubric is a good mental model).

Round types to prep:
1. **Portfolio / past-work presentation (30–45 min).** Present **1–2 case studies deeply**, not everything. Story arc with before/after metrics. Show the *decisions and tradeoffs* (why this layout over that one; the moment you changed direction), not a linear happy-path walkthrough. Present as an expert leading a discussion, not a student awaiting a grade. Strong material: a shipped AI product end-to-end, or a large enterprise service-design project.
2. **App critique (30–45 min).** Use a framework (goal of the app → user → flows → strengths/weaknesses → prioritized improvements). Pick a real app, critique structurally, tie to business goals. Show product judgment, not nitpicks.
3. **Design exercise / whiteboard or take-home.** Live whiteboard (45–60 min, narrate thinking, manage the space), on-site (~1 hr then present), or take-home (4–8 hrs, high-fidelity). They want *thinking under ambiguity and tradeoffs*, not a pretty drawing. Cover accessibility and scalability when relevant. **2026:** showing smart, disclosed use of AI tools in the exercise is a plus.
4. **Behavioral / design citizenship.** Collaboration, handling feedback, stakeholder management, advocating for users while aligning to business.

**The applicant's edge:** they *ship* — real products across multiple surfaces — and they design *for* AI. Foreground end-to-end ownership and AI experience design. Be ready for "is this person a designer or a strategist?" — answer with shipped craft, not adjectives.

## Domain 3 — Physical / Industrial Design loop

Loop typically: screen → portfolio presentation → design-process/technical interviews → sometimes a sketch test or design challenge → behavioral/culture.

Round types to prep:
1. **Portfolio walkthrough.** Tailor project selection to the company's products/industry. For each project: the problem, research, ideation/sketches, prototyping, testing, and the design decisions (incl. the direction change). The hardware hero project leads (multiple prototypes, human-factors validation, quantified outcomes — and any recognition it earned); follow with an ergonomic or research study that shows quantified anthropometric impact.
2. **Design process / technical interview.** Materials, manufacturing methods, ergonomics/human factors, cost/constraint tradeoffs. Be ready for "design within limited resources/budget" and "how you integrate ergonomic feedback." Materials TA experience, bio-inspired work, and Humanscale fluency are on-point.
3. **Sketch test / design challenge (when used).** Some firms ask for live sketching or a conceptual challenge ("design an alarm clock for the blind"–type prompts). Narrate the process; ideation breadth + reasoning over render quality.
4. **Hardware-software / UX bonus.** Increasingly, ID candidates are expected to show UX/research range. Cross-platform shipping and digital product work are a differentiator here — use them.
5. **Behavioral.** STAR; emphasize cross-disciplinary collaboration and resourcefulness under constraint.

**Positioning:** trained industrial designer who validates with real prototypes and real human-factors data, now bridging the object to its software. Use the honest-pivot reframe for formal human-factors standards (the *practice* is proven; the specific standards they'll ramp on quickly).

## Universal interview guardrails

- Every claim traces to the dossier; the pressure of a live answer is not license to inflate.
- Hold the builds-with-AI / not-an-engineer line in technical drill-downs — fluency, architecture, tradeoffs; never authorship of infrastructure code.
- Use the title-vs-capability distinction whenever a gap surfaces.
- Prep the *follow-up* depth, not just the headline answer.

## Project-specific technical Q&A prep

This section is populated during dossier intake. For each technically complex project in the applicant's portfolio — especially ones involving research methodology, materials science, biomechanics, clinical standards, or domain-specific protocols — add a sub-section here with:

- **What was actually done** (the experiment, study, or test that was run, including what was and was not measured)
- **Wording discipline** (terms the interviewer will expect precision on; common informal phrasings to avoid)
- **Anticipated follow-up questions** with model answers grounded in what the applicant can truthfully defend
- **The sharp follow-up to volunteer** (the next-experiment answer that signals seniority — shows you know the limits of your own data)
- **Product/business framing** (lead with this before the technical detail; connect the method to the outcome that matters to a hiring team)

Populate this section only with real, verified project content during the dossier build — never fabricate technical detail here.
