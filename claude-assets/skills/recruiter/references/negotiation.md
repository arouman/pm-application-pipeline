# Offer Negotiation

Goal: get the applicant the best *total* package without damaging the relationship they're about to enter. Negotiation is expected — recruiters budget for it, and candidates who negotiate well earn materially more on average than those who accept the first offer. You give the applicant the strategy and the language; they decide. **You are not a lawyer or financial advisor — this is strategy, not legal/financial advice.**

## Principles (2026)

1. **Negotiate only after a written offer, never before.** Maximum leverage is the moment they've decided they want the applicant.
2. **Counter on total comp, not base alone.** Base, equity (grant size, vesting schedule, refresh/refreshers), sign-on bonus, target bonus, level, relocation, start date, and any role-specific perks.
3. **Get the level right first.** Comp follows level. If the level is wrong, fix that before haggling dollars — a level bump usually beats a base bump.
4. **Anchor with market data, not feelings.** Use real ranges (Levels.fyi and comparable sources) and the specific value the applicant brings. Lead with data: "Based on what I'm seeing for this level and market, I was expecting total comp closer to [range] — is there room to get closer?"
5. **Never bluff a competing offer.** Recruiters are finely attuned to comp and will catch a lie; bluffing can cost the relationship. Real competing offers are the strongest lever — if the applicant has them, use them; if not, anchor on market + fit. (If more leverage is needed, the honest move is to advance 2nd/3rd-choice processes, not invent one.)
6. **Don't disclose current pay.** It's irrelevant to the role's market value and only anchors down.
7. **Have the conversation live, then document by email.** Don't negotiate solely over email; confirm agreements in writing after.
8. **One clear ask, then decide.** Make the strongest single counter rather than nickel-and-diming across many rounds. Only negotiate if the applicant is genuinely willing to commit at their number.
9. **The first counter is rarely the last word** — but stop after a reasonable round or two; don't grind a "yes" into resentment.

## Workflow

1. **Capture the full offer** — every component, in writing. Identify what's missing or vague (refresh policy? sign-on? level band?).
2. **Establish the target** — the applicant's walk-away, their target (upper half of the band for the level), and their priorities (cash now vs. equity upside vs. title/level vs. start date/relocation). Ask them; don't guess a number.
3. **Diagnose leverage** — competing offers (real only), scarcity of the applicant's profile, how far into their process the company invested, timing.
4. **Draft the counter** — anchor + the single highest-value ask + a fallback (e.g., "if base is capped, can we close the gap with a sign-on or an earlier review?").
5. **Prep responses** to yes / no / meet-in-the-middle.
6. **Run the Universal Guardrails** — value claims must trace to the dossier; never overstate scope to justify a number.

## Script fragments (adapt to the applicant's voice — short, confident, warm)

- **Buy time:** "Thank you — I'm genuinely excited about this and the team. I'd like to review the full package; can I get back to you by [date]?"
- **Anchor (data-led):** "Based on my research for this level and market, I was hoping for total comp closer to [X]. Is there flexibility there?"
- **Bridge when base is capped:** "I understand the base band. Would you be open to a sign-on to bridge the gap, a larger equity grant, or an earlier performance review?"
- **Level:** "Given the scope we discussed and my experience [specific], I think [level] is the right fit. Can we look at that?"
- **Close warm:** confirm enthusiasm, restate the one ask, give them something concrete to take back internally.

## Applicant-specific notes

- **Equity literacy is a strength** if the applicant has finance or MBA training — engage them in real terms on vesting, cliffs, refresh, and dilution rather than dumbing it down.
- **Startup/early-stage offers:** equity is often more negotiable than base; weigh growth potential and the dilution/vesting reality, not just headline numbers.
- **Relocation** may be a live lever — fold it into the package explicitly if the applicant is open to relocation.
- Hold the integrity line: the negotiation reflects the same person the materials describe. The value case is the real dossier, stated confidently — not inflated to win dollars.
